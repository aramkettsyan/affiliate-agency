You're sitting on the couch and you hear it — a long, loud gurgle coming from your dog's belly, sometimes followed by a burp, a fart, or your dog suddenly looking a little uncomfortable. It's an unsettling sound, especially the first time it happens. The good news is that a gurgling stomach (the medical term is borborygmi) is usually completely normal. The trick is knowing which gurgles are just digestion at work and which ones are your dog's gut telling you something is off.

What's actually making that noise

Those gurgles are the sound of food, liquid, and gas being pushed through the stomach and intestines by muscle contractions, a process called peristalsis. It's the exact same mechanism that makes a human stomach growl when you're hungry. Dogs have long, active digestive tracts relative to their size, and a fair amount of gas and fluid movement is a completely routine part of digestion — it's just usually quiet enough that you don't notice it over the sound of daily life.

An empty stomach is one of the most common triggers. When there's nothing to digest, the stomach and intestinal muscles still contract on their normal schedule, and with only air and stomach acid inside, the result is a hollow, gurgling sound that's often loudest right before a meal or first thing in the morning.

Everyday causes that aren't a problem

  • Hunger. A long gap between meals, or an inconsistent feeding schedule, means the stomach contracts on empty more often, which produces more noise.
  • Eating too fast. Dogs that gulp their food (or water) swallow a lot of air along with it. That extra air moves through the gut and often comes out as noise, gas, or the occasional burp.
  • A recent diet change. Switching foods abruptly, or a table-scrap treat that's richer than your dog's usual meals, can temporarily throw off digestion and cause more gurgling than usual for a day or two.
  • Normal gas production. Certain ingredients — beans, dairy, high-fiber vegetables, some commercial treats — ferment in the gut and produce gas, which you'll hear as well as smell.
  • Mild stress. Anxiety can make dogs swallow more air (from panting) and can also slow or speed up how quickly the stomach empties, both of which increase noise.

If your dog is gurgling occasionally but is otherwise bright, eating normally, and passing normal stools, this kind of everyday digestive noise usually isn't something to worry about.

Signs the gurgling is part of a bigger problem

Stomach noise becomes more concerning when it shows up alongside other symptoms rather than on its own. Watch for:

  • Vomiting, especially repeated or forceful vomiting
  • Diarrhea or noticeably softer stools than usual
  • Loss of appetite or refusing food and water
  • Lethargy, hiding, or general low energy
  • A visibly swollen, hard, or painful-to-the-touch belly
  • Straining, whining, or restlessness that seems tied to discomfort

See your veterinarian promptly if gurgling is paired with any of the above, especially a swollen or painful abdomen combined with unproductive retching — in large, deep-chested breeds this combination can signal bloat (gastric dilatation-volvulus), which is a medical emergency. Persistent gurgling with ongoing vomiting or diarrhea can also point to intestinal parasites, a food intolerance, pancreatitis, or a partial blockage from something swallowed, all of which need a vet's evaluation rather than a wait-and-see approach.

Simple changes that reduce excess gurgling and gas

For the everyday, non-emergency causes, a few practical adjustments usually make a real difference:

  • Split meals into smaller, more frequent portions. Two or three smaller meals a day keep the stomach from sitting empty for long stretches and reduce hunger-driven gurgling.
  • Slow down fast eaters. A slow-feeder bowl or a puzzle feeder forces a dog to eat more deliberately, which cuts down on the air they swallow along with their food.
  • Introduce new foods gradually. When switching diets, mix the new food in gradually over 7-10 days rather than swapping all at once, giving the gut bacteria time to adjust.
  • Watch table scraps and rich treats. Fatty, spicy, or dairy-heavy human food is one of the most common triggers for a noisy, upset stomach in dogs whose systems aren't used to it.
  • Keep water intake steady but not excessive right after meals. Gulping a large volume of water right after eating can add to gas and noise; encouraging steady sipping throughout the day is gentler on digestion.

Why some dogs stay gassy and gurgly no matter what you try

For a subset of dogs, occasional gurgling turns into an ongoing pattern — regular gas, loose stools, picky eating, or a stomach that seems sensitive to almost everything. In many of these cases, the underlying issue isn't a single trigger food but the overall balance of bacteria in the gut. When that balance is off, digestion becomes less efficient, food ferments more than it should, and the result is exactly the kind of frequent gurgling, gas, and unpredictable stool quality that owners describe as their dog just having a "sensitive stomach."

This is where a food-first approach to gut health tends to help more than switching brands of kibble over and over. Rather than guessing at another ingredient swap, it can be worth looking at what's actually supporting (or undermining) the balance of bacteria doing the digesting in the first place.

The bottom line

An occasional gurgle here and there is just your dog's digestive system doing its job, and in most healthy dogs it's nothing to worry about. Frequent, loud, or ongoing gurgling — especially alongside gas, loose stools, or a dog that seems uncomfortable after eating — is your cue to look more closely at feeding habits and gut health rather than ignore it.
