The Nesting Box Question Every New Chicken Keeper Asks

If you're planning a coop and stuck on how many nesting boxes to build, you're not alone. It's one of the first sizing questions new chicken keepers hit, right after "how big does the coop need to be." Build too few boxes and you'll get eggs laid on the floor, cracked shells, and hens squabbling over the same spot. Build too many and you've wasted materials and space that could have gone toward roosting bars or storage. The good news is that the ratio is well established and doesn't require guesswork.

The Simple Ratio: One Box Per 3-4 Hens

For a standard backyard flock, plan on one nesting box for every three to four hens. That means:

  • Up to 4 hens: 1-2 boxes
  • 5-8 hens: 2-3 boxes
  • 9-12 hens: 3-4 boxes
  • 13-20 hens: 4-6 boxes

Note that this ratio caps out in practice — even with 20+ hens, most keepers don't go far past 6-8 boxes, because hens rarely spread themselves out evenly no matter how many boxes are available. Beyond a certain point, adding boxes has diminishing returns and you're better off improving what you already have.

Why Hens Ignore Half the Boxes You Build

Here's the part that surprises a lot of new keepers: no matter how many boxes you provide, your hens will often pile into just one or two of them and ignore the rest. This is normal flock behavior, not a sign you did something wrong. Hens are drawn to whichever box feels safest, darkest, and most private, and they tend to follow whichever hen laid there first — laying is somewhat contagious within a flock. A few things that make a box more likely to actually get used:

  • Darker and more enclosed beats open and bright. Hens want privacy when laying, so boxes tucked in a dim corner get used more than ones in direct light.
  • Lower and easier to reach wins, up to a point. Boxes should sit lower than the roosting bars (so hens don't try to sleep in them) but still be elevated a few inches off the floor.
  • Bedding matters. A few inches of soft straw or pine shavings, refreshed regularly, makes a box far more appealing than bare wood.
  • Consistency counts. Once a hen picks a favorite box, don't move or reconfigure it — it can cause temporary floor-laying while she relocates her habit.

If you notice your flock crowding into one or two boxes while others sit empty, try closing off the popular box for a day or two to nudge hens toward trying the others, or simply accept it. Two well-used boxes for six hens is completely normal and not a problem to fix.

Box Size and Placement Matter More Than Box Count

A poorly sized box gets skipped even if it's technically available. For standard breed hens, aim for roughly 12 x 12 x 12 inches per box, and size up to about 14 x 14 x 14 inches if you keep larger breeds like Orpingtons or Jersey Giants. Bantams can get by with smaller boxes, around 10 x 10 x 10 inches.

A few placement rules that make a real difference:

  • Position boxes lower than the roost bars, ideally 18-20 inches off the coop floor, so hens aren't tempted to roost (and poop) in them overnight.
  • Add a small lip or threshold, about 3-4 inches high, to keep bedding and eggs from spilling out.
  • Keep boxes away from the coop's main foot traffic and food/water area — hens want a quiet spot, not a busy one.
  • If your climate gets hot, avoid boxes on a wall that takes direct afternoon sun.

Don't Forget Overall Coop and Run Space

Nesting boxes are only one piece of a coop that actually works long-term. As a general planning rule:

  • Inside the coop: 3-4 square feet of floor space per standard-size hen (2 square feet for bantams, up to 8 for large heavy breeds).
  • Outdoor run: at least 8-10 square feet per hen if they'll spend significant time in the run rather than free-ranging.
  • Roosting bar: roughly 8-10 inches of linear space per hen, positioned higher than the nesting boxes.

Undersizing the coop itself is actually a more common mistake than getting the nesting box count wrong — and it tends to show up as feather-pecking, stress, and reduced egg-laying rather than a dramatic, obvious problem. If you're designing from scratch, it's worth sizing the whole structure around your target flock size rather than building small and trying to retrofit later.

Signs You Actually Need More Boxes

Before you assume you need to build more, look for real evidence rather than just going by a formula. Genuine signs of a nesting box shortage include:

  • Multiple eggs regularly appearing on the coop floor instead of in boxes
  • Visible fighting or shoving at the boxes during peak laying hours (usually mid-morning)
  • Cracked or dirty eggs from hens standing on each other to access a single popular box
  • A hen pacing and appearing stressed while waiting for a box to open up

If none of that is happening and your hens are laying cleanly, even in just one or two boxes, your setup is working fine as-is — resist the urge to over-build.

Planning the Coop Around the Whole Flock, Not Just the Boxes

Getting nesting boxes right is a lot easier when they're planned as part of the coop's overall layout from the start — box placement relative to the roost, ventilation, predator-proofing, and the door and cleaning access all interact with each other. Trying to bolt boxes onto an already-finished coop is where most of the awkward, hard-to-clean setups come from. If you're still in the planning stage, working from a complete, tested coop design that already accounts for nesting box placement, roost height, and run sizing together will save you from the trial-and-error most first-time builders go through.
