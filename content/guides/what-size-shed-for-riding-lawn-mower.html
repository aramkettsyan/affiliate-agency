Start With the Mower, Not the Shed

The most common shed-sizing mistake is picking a number out of thin air — "I'll get a 10x10" — and discovering on delivery day that the door is too narrow, the mower has nowhere to turn, and there's no room left for a single rake. Getting the size right takes about ten minutes of measuring and a little arithmetic, and it will save you years of frustration.

This guide walks you through exactly how to size a shed for a riding lawn mower, step by step, including the details most sizing charts leave out: door width, ceiling height, ramps, and the extra gear that always ends up sharing the space.

Step 1: Measure Your Mower (the Numbers That Actually Matter)

Grab a tape measure before you do anything else. You need three numbers from your mower:

  • Overall width: Measure from the outermost edge of the left tire to the outermost edge of the right tire. On most residential riding mowers this lands between 42 and 54 inches. Zero-turn mowers are often a few inches wider.
  • Overall length: From the front of the hood to the back of the seat or rear tire — typically 60 to 80 inches for a standard tractor, and up to 90 inches or more for larger zero-turns.
  • Height at the tallest point: Usually the top of the ROPS (the roll-over protection bar) or the steering wheel, whichever is higher. Most fall in the 42–50 inch range without the ROPS raised, but folded ROPS can add several more inches if you keep it up during storage.

Write these down. They are the floor for every other decision you make.

Step 2: Add Working Space Around the Mower

The mower's footprint is not the shed's footprint. You need room to:

  • Walk alongside the mower to check tires, oil, and blades
  • Open panels or the hood for maintenance without hitting a wall
  • Swing the steering wheel all the way to each side when driving in and out

A reliable rule of thumb: add at least 18 inches to each side of the mower's width, and at least 24 inches in front and behind. That gives you a minimum interior footprint of roughly mower width + 36 inches by mower length + 48 inches.

Example: a mower that is 48 inches wide and 72 inches long needs a minimum clear interior of about 84 inches wide by 120 inches long — which works out to roughly 7 feet by 10 feet of unobstructed floor space.

Practical Shed Size Recommendations by Mower Type

Here is how those numbers map to standard shed dimensions for mower-only storage:

  • Small riding tractor (42–44" deck): An 8x10 shed works but leaves almost no room for anything else. Doors must be at least 60 inches wide.
  • Mid-size tractor (46–54" deck): A 10x12 is the sweet spot for mower-only storage and fits most suburban yards. You gain space for a few hand tools on the walls.
  • Large tractor or small zero-turn: Step up to a 10x16 or 12x16. You will have enough floor space to store the mower, pull it out for maintenance, and still keep a workbench or shelving unit along one wall.
  • Large zero-turn (60"+ deck): A 12x20 or larger gives you genuine working space and room for seasonal attachments like a bagger, snow plow blade, or spreader.

Step 3: Account for Everything Else Going In There

Almost nobody stores only a mower in their shed. Before you settle on a size, make a quick inventory of everything that will share the space:

  • Push mower (add roughly 2x3 feet of floor space)
  • Snow blower (add roughly 2.5x3 feet)
  • Gas cans, oil, and fluids (a shelf works; don't leave them on the floor)
  • Long-handled tools: rakes, shovels, brooms (wall-mounted holders keep these off the floor)
  • Hoses, sprinklers, and irrigation equipment
  • Seasonal attachments for the mower (bagger, snow plow, spreader)
  • Bicycles, wagons, or outdoor kids' toys

If your list is longer than five items, size up at least once. A 10x12 that feels spacious with just the mower becomes a maze once the snow blower, push mower, and garden tools arrive.

The Two Details That Catch People Off Guard

Door Width

Many pre-built sheds come with a standard single door that is 36 inches wide. A 48-inch riding mower cannot pass through a 36-inch door. You need a double door or a single wide door with a clear opening at least 6 inches wider than your mower — which typically means a finished opening of 54 to 60 inches minimum, and 72 inches if you have a large zero-turn.

If you are building the shed yourself, planning for a 6-foot double door from the start is far cheaper than modifying a finished opening later. Always confirm the actual clear opening width, not the door panel width — the frame and hinges eat a few inches.

The Ramp

A riding mower cannot roll over a threshold. You need a ramp that bridges the gap between the ground and the shed floor, and that ramp needs to be wide enough to match your mower's tire track, non-slip, and rated for the mower's weight (most residential tractors weigh 400–700 lbs; larger zero-turns can hit 1,000 lbs or more).

A ramp that is 6 to 8 feet long with a gradual slope keeps the belly of the mower from scraping. Steeper is faster to build but harder on the deck. If you are designing the shed from scratch, setting the floor at or very near ground level eliminates the ramp problem entirely — a common detail in plans designed specifically for mower storage.

Ceiling Height

Standard shed walls are 7 feet tall before you account for the roof pitch. That is enough for most mowers with the ROPS folded down, but if you plan to store the mower with the ROPS raised — or if you want a loft or overhead storage above the mower — confirm the interior clearance first. A 7-foot wall height with a gable roof gives you closer to 9–10 feet at the peak, which allows for useful overhead storage on either side without affecting mower clearance in the center.

Should You Build or Buy?

Pre-built sheds are convenient but come in fixed sizes that rarely match your actual mower footprint and door needs perfectly. Building your own lets you set the door width where you need it, pitch the floor to avoid water pooling, add a ramp into the foundation design, and choose dimensions based on your specific mower rather than a manufacturer's catalog.

It is also substantially cheaper for the same square footage, and far more satisfying. The catch is having reliable plans to follow — shed framing looks simple until you are staring at a pile of lumber trying to figure out how the roof rafters join the wall plates.

If you have been considering the DIY route, having a library of plans designed for exactly this kind of purpose — storage sheds sized for specific equipment, with proper door frames, flooring, and rooflines already worked out — takes most of the guesswork off the table. That is worth looking into before you price out lumber or pick a kit.
