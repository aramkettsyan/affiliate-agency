Switching a dog from kibble to homemade meals can look simple on paper and messy in practice. Do it too fast and you get loose stools, picky eating, or a dog that refuses both bowls. Do it carefully and most dogs settle into the new diet within about a week or two. Here is a practical transition plan that keeps digestion stable and expectations realistic.

Why a gradual switch matters

A dog's gut microbiome adapts to whatever it has been eating regularly. Kibble and homemade food differ in moisture, fat, fiber, and how quickly they move through the gut. An overnight swap dumps a new workload on that system all at once, which is why sudden changes so often show up as diarrhea, gas, or appetite dips. A phased mix lets the gut catch up while your dog still recognizes part of every meal.

This is especially important for puppies, seniors, and dogs with a history of sensitive stomachs. Those dogs can still transition successfully — they just need a slower ramp and closer watching for soft stool or vomiting.

A simple 7–10 day transition schedule

  • Days 1–2: About 75% current food and 25% homemade. Serve at the usual meal times so the routine stays familiar.
  • Days 3–4: Roughly 50/50. If stools stay firm and your dog finishes meals eagerly, keep moving. If stools soften, stay at this ratio an extra day or two.
  • Days 5–6: About 25% old food and 75% homemade.
  • Day 7+ (up to day 10 for sensitive dogs): Full homemade meals once stools and appetite look normal.

If your dog skips meals, vomits more than once, or has watery diarrhea, step back one stage for 48 hours before increasing the homemade share again. Persistent vomiting, blood in stool, lethargy, or refusal to drink are reasons to pause the transition and call your veterinarian.

How to portion homemade food so your dog stays satisfied

Homemade meals are denser in moisture than most kibble, so a cup-for-cup swap by volume usually underfeeds. Aim for calories rather than scoop size. A common starting point for adult maintenance is roughly 25–30 calories per pound of body weight per day, then adjust for activity, age, and body condition. Split that total into the same number of meals your dog already eats.

Weigh your dog before you start and again after two weeks. Ribs should be easy to feel under a thin fat layer, and you should see a clear waist from above. If the waist disappears, trim portions; if ribs become sharp and energy drops, increase slightly.

What belongs in a balanced homemade bowl

  • A clear protein base such as cooked chicken, turkey, lean beef, or fish your dog already tolerates.
  • Cooked carbohydrates like rice, sweet potato, or oats if your recipes include them — plain and fully cooked.
  • Dog-safe vegetables such as carrots, green beans, or pumpkin for fiber and bulk.
  • Essential nutrients that meat and veg alone usually miss — especially calcium and certain vitamins and fats. Unbalanced homemade diets are a real long-term risk, so use a complete recipe from a trusted source or a vet-formulated supplement plan rather than improvising from leftovers.

Never feed cooked bones, onion, garlic, grapes, raisins, xylitol, excessive fat trimmings, or raw dough. When in doubt about an ingredient, leave it out and check with your vet.

Common transition problems and quick fixes

  • Soft stool for a day or two: Normal for many dogs. Hold the current mix ratio, add a spoon of plain canned pumpkin (not pie filling), and wait before increasing homemade food.
  • Dog picks out the new food: Warm the homemade portion slightly and mix thoroughly so every bite smells the same. Avoid adding people-food toppers that teach selective eating.
  • Dog refuses the old kibble once homemade appears: Serve smaller, more frequent meals and keep the transition moving rather than bargaining with treats.
  • Extra gas: Often temporary as gut bacteria shift. Reduce rich fats and novel proteins until things settle.

When homemade is a good fit — and when it is not

Homemade feeding works well for owners who can cook consistently, measure portions, and follow complete recipes. It is a weaker fit if your schedule is unpredictable, your dog has complex medical diet needs you have not reviewed with a vet, or you are not willing to track calcium and other essential nutrients. Some dogs with allergies do better on carefully chosen homemade diets; others need a veterinary prescription diet instead. That decision belongs with your veterinarian, especially if your dog has kidney, liver, pancreas, or urinary issues.
